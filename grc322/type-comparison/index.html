Didot vs. Bodoni

History of each typeface

Didot

Didot is classified as a Modern or Didone typeface and was first designed in the late 18th century (1784 – 1811) in Paris by Firmin Didot, a French painter and engraver. The typeface itself was based on the physical type that Didot and his brother cut, cast, and used for printing. Didot was heavily influenced by the Baskerville type when creating this typeface, and it features strong vertical stress, with extreme thicks and thins, giving a sleek and professional appearance. While this makes Didot stand out against the page at large sizes, it can make the font unsuitable for body text, as smaller sizing can cause certain letters to become difficult to read. In contrast, Didot excels as a display font. Since its creation, Didot has been revived and altered many times, yet it continues to remain a highly recognizable and relevant typeface.

Bodoni

Bodoni is also classified as a Modern or Didone typeface, and was also designed in the late 18th century (1798) in Parma by Giambattista Bodoni, an Italian printer and type designer. Immediately after its creation, the typeface was primarily used by Bodoni’s own printing office, with book printing as its main outlet. Bodoni was influenced heavily by both the Baskerville and Didot typefaces when creating this typeface. The similarities between the two fonts are evident, with Bodoni also featuring high vertical stress and extreme thicks and thins on its letterforms. Despite these similarities, Bodoni remains inarguably its own font, with many stylistic differences that set it apart from Didot. Bodoni has also been revisited since its creation, with many other forms of the typeface being created, and today, it has also become a well-known and widely used typeface representative of classic type styles and elegance.
